Planning & Process


To begin this 1-week design sprint for class, I interviewed users about their experience with health insurance. I probed specifically on how they pick health insurance, on what criteria they evaluate it, and their confidence understanding it.


I expected to hear that people wanted an easier way to compare insurance plans or understand the terms better, but to my surprise my research led me to a different conclusion. Users wanted cost transparency, were overwhelmed when thinking about their health insurance, and did not want to spend time thinking or dealing with their insurance. While I personally would have found a different question more interesting to solve, I stayed true to my research finding. Users wanted a simple app to find a doctor who accepts their insurance (see affinity map below).


With my first round of user research complete, I was able to define the problem I was going to solve with the Doctor Detective app:

A person seeking a new physician needs a way to easily look up which doctors accept his/her insurance because currently it is overwhelming and difficult to figure out where to look.

Before jumping into prototypes, I created a user flow mapping the possible paths and decisions points. I also sketched a storyboard, representing one of the ways Doctor Detective would improve users' lives.

I was now ready to start creating a prototype. I began with a lo-fi paper sketch with just blocks. This format let me quickly and easily compare different layouts.


The first round of paper sketches proved to be generally easy to follow for users. To upload their insurance details, I thought taking a picture of their insurance card would be easy for them. The backend code could decipher the necessary details. Upon testing, this seemed intuitive, although one user pointed out that military insurance does not offer cards. I therefore added an option to manually enter your insurance information. Insurance is confusing, so I also added info help buttons.



After a round of testing the paper prototype, I redrew the sketches and uploaded them into Marvel app. I completed a second round of user testing with the prototype on my phone.




Final Project


After a busy week, I had a working paper model, which I uploaded to Marvel app. A few key screens are depicted after the next steps, or you can view it my through the Marvel app. I also spent an extra afternoon mocking up a higher fidelity higher fidelity prototype, and linked it in InVision.


I considered some next steps to take the app to the next level.

  • Add search functionality to compare if different insurance plans cover a physician
  • Partner with a physician rating service and include physician ratings as part of the search criteria
    • At this time, physician rating services are not robust or accurate
  • After including a rating system, could also offer hospitals and offices to advertise their practice
    • While this would help the app earn money, it is unethical to promote certain listings without a separate accompanying set of ratings
  • Add additional search criteria such as physician gender and associated hospital
    • Features would be defined after another round of user research
  • Market to offices and hospitals to share our app with their patients

A few of the unique screens are below, or you can view the interactive prototype on the Marvel app. The specialty physician search had a drop down that could be scrolled, or the user could start typing to speed through the selection process and jump to areas within the dropdown. It was important to me that there was a dropdown and display of options, since spelling specialities can be challenging for users.

To combat errors, two additional designs decisions were include in the search by name category. One was to include an error page in case a physician's name could not be found, and the other was to list a physician and note that the insurance was not accepted. This reassures the user that the app is functioning, since the name they searched was found, but still notifies them that their insurance would not be accepted.

Finally, viewing a physician also linked to the user's phone default maps, phone, and browser if they clicked into the map, phone number, or web address.

I spent an extra afternoon mocking up medium and high fidelity prototypes in Sketch. It was important to me that the screens remain very simple so as to not further overwhelm the user. To seem accessible yet trustworthy, I stuck with a sans-serif font and a blue palette. For my high fidelity screens, I linked them together and created a clickable prototype in InVision.
