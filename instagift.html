Instagift

Added new concept feature to Instagram


∨

Overview


Goals
  • Diversify Instagram revenue stream while maintaining brand image
  • Add a new concept feature to the Instagram app to enable users to print their photos onto merchandise (photo gifts)
  • Completed prototype and presentation within 2 week design sprint for UXDI assignment
Key Challenges
  • Followed Instagram design guidelines
  • Add to the app without disrupting the current components
  • Design to meet business and user goals
  • Determine appropriate symbols to guide user through the added feature

Research & Discovery


|| Background

As an assigment for the UXDI course, my partner and I were tasked to diversify Instagram's revenue stream while its maintaining brand image. We had to work to add it to the existing app without disrupting the current features that are essential to Instagram. Our addition would enable users to customize and purchase physical versions of their photos to share with friends and family; this is otherwise known as a photo gift.

Before conducting user research or looking at competitors, we wanted to look at Instagram's business background. We used the demographic information to guide our user research, so that we would interview a representative mix of candidates. We found that 55% of current users were 18-29 year olds, and about 65% were female.

|| Competitor Analysis

We also wanted to understand the current photo gift landscape so we could ask more informed questions during our user research. We analyzed other photo gift apps, some of which included integration with Instagram. We performed a heuristic evaluation on Walgreens, Snapfish, Photo Bucket, and Social Print Studio. We analyzed each competitor on several characteristics, rating each aspect on a scale of 1 (poor) to 5 (excellent):

  • Integration with Instagram
  • Platform accessibility i.e. iOS and/or android apps
  • Paper print options such as posters and cards
  • Other print items such as mugs and magnets
  • Price
  • Shipping
  • Simplicity

The heuristic analysis proved Snapfish to be the top competitor. It was important for us to take remember what Snapfish did well, so that Instagram's photo gift feature could compete with it.

|| User Research

We sent out a survey to recruit users who fit the demographic criteria we had discovered from our business research. We spoke with users who were within the age criteria, as well as an even mix of males of females. One of the users was also a professional photographer, as we wanted to explore that viewpoint, as well. We asked users about their experience with photography and photo editing (including Instagram), their frequency of Instgram use, their experience with photo gifts, and their attitudes toward ecommerce.

After organizing our user research, we created an affinity map to identify the key themes:

  • People do not document their lives with Instagram, they capture happy moments sporadically
  • People would buy prints of these moments for loved ones, but not for themselves
  • People want a quick and easy purchase process

Although we thought users would want to print their photos to hang in their apartments, our research proved our hypothesis to be mistaken.

We learned that users would want to print their photos to send as gifts to family or friends, not for themselves. They do not want to hang their own photos in their homes. They want to share tokens of their shared moments. This finding would help shape our decisions about which products to offer in our design and how we framed it in the app. The feature should be a gift for others, not a standard merchandise purchase for oneself.

|| Contextual Inquiry & Task Analysis

To help hone in on what makes a photo gift app great, we conducted a contextual inquiry with the top competitor, Snapfish. We had participants walk through photo and product selection, and walk through the purchasing process up until they had to input credit card information. We learned there were a few areas of improvement for Snapfish which we wanted to address in our design:

  • An overwhelming number of product categories
  • Icons on the product page were confusing
  • Product specifications were hidden
  • Entering the shipping address was confusing

To complete our research, we also conducted a task analysis. There were a few key takeaways:

  • Pick the photo and product to match the person and occasion
  • Quality of the product is important
  • The "gift" aspect should be reflected throughout the process
  • Checkout was complex and burdensome

Rapid Prototyping & Testing

|| Paper Prototype

To imitate the clean cut lines and white space of Instagram, we copied components of our sketches in Illustrator and wrote the text in the program instead of by hand. We still moved paper pieces around during testing, but this version of a paper prototype more closely imitated the feel of Instagram.

As we did for our user research, contextual inquiries, and task analyses, we again chose users to test with who were representative of Instagram's demographics. Users liked how seamless and easy to use it was: "If you use Instagram, you could use this." There were a few areas of improvement:

  • Some of the icons were confusing, particularly on the product page to access the editing features
  • The buy call-to-action was not clear
  • The Instagift feature was missing a compelling unique selling proposition (USP)

We incorporated the user feedback and iterated on our design. We went back to our design goals to improve the USP.

Users love Instagram's hashtags - they are fun, unique, and capture the moment. Users also liked to leave messages for photo gifts, to really make them personalized. We decided to include a text editor where you could write a message and include hashtags. We also wanted to clear up the icons so that users knew how to swap out the photo on their product, could preview it on a 360° view of the product, and re-edit the photo as needed. This brought the user back to the main reason why they liked Instagram: hashtags and editing photos.

|| Medium-Fidelity Prototypes

We updated icons, verbiage, and the buy call-to-action for our medium fidelity prototype. We created the prototype in Sketch, and synced it in Invision to test with users. Thinking about the sustainability from the back-end, we wanted to limit the number of characters a message could be for each type of product. This would improve the 360° preview, as well as the back-end production.

We again heard positive feedback on our integration with the brand, as well as the smooth checkout process. We had tried to incorporate iOS slide-to-delete feature for checkout, but none of our iPhone users understood to slide. For the high-fidelity, we decided to update it to include a standard button ("remove" or "delete") that would remove an item from the cart.

The updated icons on the product page were still ambigious. We would have to iterate them again.

|| High-Fidelity Prototypes

The high-fidelity prototype also continued to receive great feedback on smooth brand integration and check-out. Users also liked the ability to re-edit the photo specifically for the photo gift. The photo editing and ability to add text and hashtags finally created a compelling unique selling proposition. Users thought it was fun!

"This would make me buy a photo gift."

Users noted that their expectations behind the edit button were different than its outcomes. We still had to work to improve the icons! For our final prototype, we wanted to update the screen that appeared when the icon was clicked.



Result


The updated interactive high-fidelity prototype is linked on Invision.

View Interactive Hi-Fi Prototype

Among other things, we had updated the icons, the space on the text input, the character marker, the call-to-action, the interaction to remove items from the cart, and the USP. We now had an updated high-fidelity prototype the users thought was fun and fit with Instagram's look and feel. We followed the brand guidelines, and users responded to Instagift's smooth integration with the Instagram app.

We added to the email confirmation a time-limited offer for the gift-giver and gift-recipient to get $1.00 off their next order. This would encourage users to continue to use Instagift and support its initial growth.

We also defined our product offering by researching the top competitors' best selling products to make sure Instagift would have a competitive product offering. We limited the number of products, however, so that the decision process would not be overwhelming like Snapfish's.

|| Next Steps

The prototype received positive feedback after we presented it to an outside panel. There were still areas of improvement that we would have liked to implement if we had continued the project for another design sprint.

  • Optimize user flow for editing text
  • A/B test edit icons
  • Add a way to access address from Contacts when entering shipping information
  • Test saved payment profiles

There were also future enhancements we would like to incorporate into the product:

  • Order History in profile so you can view past gifts you have sent, similar to how you view posted photos
  • More product variants
  • More text options, such as editing font and color
